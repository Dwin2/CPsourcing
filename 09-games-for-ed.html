3. Product Overview

Games for Ed designs low-tech board and card games aligned to India's K-12 national curriculum, targeting five core skills: communication, problem-solving, teamwork, logical thinking, and multitasking. The games are specifically built for classrooms with minimal technology access, and the programme includes teacher training on game-based pedagogy.

5. How I Found the Company

I found Games for Ed through a Harvard Crimson article from October 2025 profiling HGSE alumni who were building education ventures in emerging markets. The co-founder Mridula Chalamalasetti's HGSE '25 affiliation was confirmed through the LDIT programme page. I was drawn to this venture because it addresses a fundamentally different challenge than most edtech: delivering learning outcomes in low-resource, low-connectivity environments where digital tools simply don't work. India's K-12 market (260 M+ students) is the world's largest, and the "low-tech for low-resource" approach is refreshingly contrarian in an edtech landscape obsessed with AI and digital platforms.

Strengths

  • Counter-cyclical to edtech hype: While most edtech startups require devices and connectivity, Games for Ed works in classrooms with zero technology. This "low-tech" approach means no infrastructure dependency, near-zero per-student distribution cost (a board game costs under $2 to produce at scale), and resilience against the digital-divide critique that plagues many edtech ventures.
  • Strong early traction and evidence: A 28% average improvement in classroom metrics, successful teacher training programmes, and enthusiastic school testimonials provide early evidence of efficacy — crucial for an education venture seeking to scale through school district partnerships or government adoption.
Ask AI to verify or expand

Weaknesses

  • Unclear revenue model and scalability: With only a $5K grant and no disclosed venture funding, the venture's business model is uncertain. Selling low-cost physical games to price-sensitive Indian schools may struggle to generate the unit economics that attract venture capital. The venture may be better suited as a social enterprise or NGO than a VC-backed startup.
  • Limited moat: Board and card games, once designed, can be easily copied or replicated by local manufacturers. Without IP protection, brand defensibility, or network effects, a successful product could be cloned by better-resourced competitors or even by the schools themselves.

7. Five Questions for the Team

  1. What is your business model — do you sell games directly to schools, to government education departments, or through a B2B distributor?
  2. How do you protect your IP when the games can be physically replicated? Is the moat in the game design itself, the teacher training programme, or the curriculum alignment?
  3. What is your path to reaching 1,000+ schools — is it through individual school sales, state government adoption, or partnerships with education NGOs like Pratham or Teach For India?
  4. Have you considered digitising the game designs for schools that do have device access, creating a hybrid physical/digital product line?
  5. Are you structured as a for-profit company or a non-profit/social enterprise, and what type of capital (venture, impact, philanthropic) are you seeking?
