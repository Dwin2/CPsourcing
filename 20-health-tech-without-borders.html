20. Health Tech Without Borders

Humanitarian Telehealth & Digital Health Resources for Crisis Regions

1. Startup Name

Health Tech Without Borders (HTWB)

2. Founder Contact

Dr. Jarone Lee, MD, MPH — Co-Founder

Associate Professor, Harvard Medical School; Director of Global Partnerships, MGB Global Advisory; Vice Chief of Critical Care, Mass General Hospital

LinkedIn: Health Tech Without Borders on LinkedIn

Website: htwb.org

3. Product Overview

Health Tech Without Borders is a global nonprofit leveraging digital tools and telehealth technology to provide immediate access to medical and psychological support for people affected by humanitarian crises. The organization connects patients in crisis regions with remote clinicians and provides training for local healthcare workers.

4. Funding Stage

Grant-Funded Nonprofit — Founded 2022. Has served 65,000+ patients and trained 2,000+ clinicians. Clinton Global Initiative commitment participant (2023). Partner with VSee Health and iDocta on Project VITAL Africa in Cameroon. Non-sectarian and apolitical by design.

5. How I Found the Company

I discovered Health Tech Without Borders through the Harvard Global Health Institute, which profiled Dr. Jarone Lee's work on health tech innovation. The founder's credentials — Harvard Medical School Associate Professor, Mass General Hospital Vice Chief of Critical Care, and over a decade of disaster response experience — provide exceptional clinical credibility. The organization's impact (65,000+ patients, 2,000+ clinicians trained) demonstrates meaningful scale in humanitarian health.

Strengths

  • World-class medical credibility: Founded by a Harvard Medical School professor and MGH critical care leader with 10+ years of disaster response experience. This pedigree opens doors to partnerships, grants, and volunteer clinicians that few organizations can match.
  • High-profile institutional partnerships: Clinton Global Initiative commitment, partnerships with VSee Health and iDocta, and collaboration with Harvard Global Health Institute provide legitimacy and potential funding pathways.

Weaknesses

  • Nonprofit model with grant dependency: Serving crisis regions is inherently difficult to monetize. The organization depends on grants and donations, which can be volatile and tied to donor priorities and geopolitical attention cycles.
  • Operational complexity in crisis zones: Providing telehealth in humanitarian emergencies faces infrastructure challenges (internet connectivity, power), security concerns, and rapidly changing conditions. Sustained service delivery is extremely difficult.

7. Five Questions for the Team

  1. What is your funding model — foundation grants, government contracts, or corporate philanthropy — and what is your runway?
  2. How do you handle the connectivity and infrastructure challenges inherent in crisis regions?
  3. What clinical outcomes data do you collect, and how do you measure the quality of care delivered remotely?
  4. How do you recruit and retain volunteer clinicians? What is your clinician engagement and retention rate?
  5. Are there opportunities to expand beyond crisis response to underserved regions more broadly — or is the humanitarian focus core to your mission?
